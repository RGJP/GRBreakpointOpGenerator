"BOUNDING OVERWATCH" An essential movement tactic Bounding overwatch is a tactic used to move across terrain while maintaining constant security. In this method, one or more team members advance while others provide cover (overwatch) from a secure position. The team then "bounds" by having the overwatching element move forward to a new position, while the advancing team takes over the overwatch role. This approach is more effective than having everyone move at the same time because it reduces exposure to enemy fire. With bounding overwatch, only a portion of the team is vulnerable during each phase of movement, ensuring that there are always individuals in a position to observe, suppress, or engage threats, thus enhancing the team's safety and tactical flexibility. To clarify further: The 4-person team splits into two pairs (A and B for demonstration). Pair B moves while Pair A provides stationary cover. Then Pair A moves as Pair B covers, alternating in this leapfrog manner. https://imgur.com/a/bounding-overwatch-turn-audio-on-EqWq9l1 (edited)

NEUTRALIZING THREATS AS A TEAM When spotting and engaging hostiles, it is crucial that all four squad members are in perfect sync. We never fire unless the team leader gives the green light, and we always communicate our status immediately to teammates. Here’s a simple rundown: While patrolling, if the pointman spots three enemies, he might say: "Eyes on three hostiles, 4 o'clock." Then, each teammate responds by indicating which target they are getting ready to engage: "2: Holding left." "3: Holding center." "4: Holding right." Once the team leader hears that all members are holding their targets and are ready to execute, he might give a command like: "3, 2, 1, execute." or "Red, Yellow, Green." At that point, shots are fired, and the threat is neutralized quickly and in sync. Simple as that. As a teammate, if you can't see the enemies, you can say 'Standby, getting visual.' Once you have a target in sight, specify which one you're holding, for example, 'Holding right.' (edited)

MSR (Main Supply Route / Road) Crossings:
  • Always maintain situational awareness on both sides of the road. One person watches the 9 o'clock position, another covers 3 o'clock. As teammates cross, they do the same for each other. Once everyone has crossed, get off the road and revert to the previous formation.
Demolition:
  • Generally, use one C4 charge per target, unless dealing with heavily armored targets (e.g., drones). If possible, bundle targets together (e.g., three vehicles) and use one charge to disable them all, rather than using multiple charges separately.
Detonation Protocol:
  • Never detonate without clearance from the team leader.
  • Even with clearance, double-check that everyone is clear of the blast zone or has confirmed radio contact that they are safe (e.g., overwatch position on a distant hill with radio confirmation).
Safety Considerations:
  • Be aware of secondary explosions. Avoid getting close to flaming vehicles, as they are prone to secondary explosions (vehicles explode, catch fire, and then explode again).
Detonation Warning:
  • When detonating, clearly announce: "DET in 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 — FIRE IN THE HOLE! FIRE IN THE HOLE!" so everyone is aware of the impending blast.

Navigation:
  • When providing navigation to your driver or pilot, use clear terms and avoid waiting until the last second to indicate turns or maneuvers. Always refer to your TACMAP for navigation and not the road—your teammates will handle security. Keep your instructions short but clear, and always give directions in advance.
Good Example: "Keep following the road... in about 500 meters, take a HARD LEFT onto the dirt road. ...coming up, HARD LEFT in 3...2...1... HARD LEFT." Bad Example: "...turn left at the road" (When? Where? What kind of road?) "TURN LEFT NOW! TURN LEFT!" (No advance indication, driver has no time to react.) Aircraft Navigation:
  • In an aircraft, if the pilot requires navigation, provide instructions like: "Adjust 30 degrees right." You can also give helpful information such as: "Got eyes on the target objective, 2 o'clock, 1 klick out (1 kilometer)."

Essential Loud Firefight Tactics: In addition to stealth and precision, operators must be prepared for loud firefights, a key part of many missions. These situations require swift and effective action to ensure team safety and mission success. Loud engagements typically occur in two scenarios: 1. Planned Engagements: The team initiates a high-intensity firefight after strategically setting up an ambush, using loud tactics to quickly neutralize enemies with maximum force and speed, minimizing enemy reaction time. 2. Unplanned Engagements: The team is unexpectedly detected during movement, forcing them to respond to a sudden firefight from a less-than-ideal position. Regardless of the situation, certain principles must always be followed for effective management of loud firefights.

1. Shift into gear and get to a better position FAST: When combat begins, switch from slow, methodical stealth mode to rapid action. Quickly move to hard cover like buildings or rocks, even if the position isn't ideal. Staying exposed makes you vulnerable, so prioritize defensive positions to control the chaos. 2. Maintain situational awareness and communication: Ensure every team member covers a sector to prevent flanking, and keep communication brief but clear. Continuous verbal updates are crucial for survival, and always be aware of threats from above or below, depending on your position. 3. Anticipate and be quick: Prepare for all possible threats, like grenades, RPGs, snipers, and more. Anticipating these dangers helps you react calmly and quickly, avoiding panic and increasing your chances of survival. Stay mentally ready for any scenario to prevent being caught off guard. 4. Break the enemy's advance: Use grenades, flashbangs, or EMPs strategically to disrupt the enemy's movement and hinder their ability to rush your position. This tactic not only slows their progress but can also disable drones or other electronic equipment, providing a critical buffer zone that gives you time to regroup or set up defenses. 5. Stay put and return fire: During a firefight, avoid unnecessary movement, as most injuries come from exposure while relocating. If you must move, wait for a moment of reduced intensity and request suppressive fire from teammates to cover your shift. Ideally, let the enemy approach your position while using slight adjustments to stay unpredictable. Use short bursts of fire and limit exposure from cover, always employing a cover/return fire strategy. In intense combat, don't hesitate to use blind fire for suppression, and throw grenades without exposing yourself to minimize risk to you and your team. (edited)

6. Injured teammate protocol: Prioritize team safety over rushing to aid a wounded teammate. Cover the medic and wait for a less intense moment to assist. An additional essential concept: The P.A.C.E. planning method ensures mission success through layered contingency plans. Whatever the objective, "P.A.C.E. yourself" by preparing multiple layers of plans instead of relying on a single approach.
    Primary: The preferred method for achieving the objective, designed for efficiency and reliability under normal conditions. Alternate: A viable second option when the primary becomes unfeasible, typically using different routes, resources, or methods. Contingency: A less optimal but still functional approach that accounts for significant disruptions, often requiring improvisation. Designed to achieve the objective under degraded or challenging conditions. Emergency: The last resort, ensuring survival or mission continuity under extreme circumstances when all other plans fail.
      P.A.C.E planning enhances resilience and adaptability in our operations. In a nutshell, it inserts into the phases we must do for the highest probability of mission success and cohesion: INFIL RECON P.A.C.E. EXECUTION EXFIL "Come prepared, leave spared"       (edited)
